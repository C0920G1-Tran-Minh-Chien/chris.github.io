Writing in class 1

“Make lots of friends, overcome cultural barriers, and learn from them”—that’s what my father told me at the airport before I boarded my flight to the US. So, let’s see who I am before you decide to be friends with me.

My full name is Tran Minh Chien, but you can call me Chris. I’m from Da Nang City, Vietnam. I graduated from Da Nang University of Science and Technology with a major in Electronics and Telecommunications, studying there from 2016 to 2022.

It’s a difficult major; we had to study both hardware and software, along with a bit of deep learning and AI. During this time, I wasn’t focused on studying, so my grades were quite poor.

But in my final year at university, I was lucky to find my first job, right in the middle of the COVID-19 outbreak.

My job was to teach programming and everything related to it. It was quite a challenging job for someone as new as me. I had to stand in front of a crowd to teach classes, correct assignments, and explain detail to them, even though at that time, I didn’t have any real experience working on an actual project.

But luckily, I adapted very well. I shared everything I knew with my students, and it seemed like they appreciated it.

It’s quite funny that two of my former university classmates becoming my students here.

After nearly two years, I wanted to work on larger projects and learn from real-world experience, so I decided to leave and look for a new job. I interviewed at a few companies and was lucky enough to get offers from all of them. This time, I chose a Japanese tech company. I got this job after going through three interview rounds that lasted a total of six hours.

Cross-Cultural Tips for International Students: Communication

Cross-Cultural Tips for International Students: Communication Student: Chris Institutional Affiliation: Suffolk University Course: AE052 Graduate Academic Success Instructor: Dr. Rachel Toncelli Date: 03/03/2025 Hu (2018) gives helpful advice to international students on how to communicate better in a new cultural environtment, especially in Western education. The article talks about the differences between direct and indirect communication and encourages students to be flexible when talking to professors and classmates. It also highlights the important of listening, understanding body language, and being aware of cultural differences. Additionally, Hu (2018) suggests that students ask if they don’t understand something and be patient as they improve their communication skills. By practicing and being open-minded, international students can adapt more easily, build relationships, and have a better experience studying abroad. References Hu, D. (2018, August 3).
